› why do you think log scale for saleprice doesn't help?


• Most likely because your evaluation is RMSE on the original dollar scale, not on
  log(SalePrice).

  When you train on log1p(SalePrice), OLS optimizes squared error in log space.
  That changes the objective:

  - It cares more about relative error than absolute dollar error.
  - It penalizes missing a $100k house by 20% about as much as missing a $500k
    house by 20%.

  But prepare.py:4 scores plain RMSE in dollars, so large absolute misses on
  expensive homes matter a lot. Log-target models often improve percentage-style
  fit while getting worse on absolute-dollar RMSE.

  A few specific reasons it likely hurt here:

  - Your current best model already handles nonlinearity partly through regime
    splitting in train.py, so one big reason to log the target is already reduced.
  - Back-transforming with expm1 introduces bias. OLS on logs estimates the mean
    of log-price, not the mean price itself, so naive expm1(pred) tends to
    underpredict high-end homes unless you add a correction.
  - Ames has influential premium neighborhoods and high-quality homes. On
    original-scale RMSE, fitting those expensive tails well is valuable. Log scale
    compresses them.

  So the short version is: log(SalePrice) is often good when you care about
  multiplicative error, stability, or percentage accuracy. Your benchmark rewards
  absolute dollar accuracy, and this dataset seems to benefit more from modeling
  price levels directly.

› why rmse improves by r2 drops


• Because these two metrics are measuring different things, and in your current
  setup the reported r2 is not the validation r2 for the final dollar predictions.

  In train.py, you currently print:

  - RMSE on validation data, after converting predictions back to dollar scale
  - average of model_low.rsquared and model_high.rsquared

  That r2 value is:

  - in-sample
  - computed in log-price space
  - averaged across two separate regime models
  - not evaluated on the validation predictions that RMSE uses

  So it is completely possible for:

  - validation RMSE in dollars to improve
  - printed r2 to drop

  Those are not the same target.

  There’s also a deeper reason: once you switched to log1p(SalePrice) with
  smearing correction, the model is optimized in log space, but judged by RMSE in
  dollar space. A change can slightly reduce fit in log space while improving
  large-dollar prediction errors after back-transformation, which lowers RMSE.

  If you want the metrics to line up better, the right fix is to report a
  validation r2 on the final dollar predictions, for example using:
  r2_score(np.expm1(y_val), y_pred)

  Then you’d be comparing both metrics on the same held-out predictions.

› what is more important for accuracy rmse or r-squared


• RMSE.

  In this project, prepare.py:4 evaluates with RMSE, and program.md says to
  optimize the lowest RMSE. So for actual model quality here, r2 is secondary.

  Why:

  - RMSE is the benchmark you are judged on.
  - RMSE is in dollars, so it tells you the typical prediction error size.
  - r2 is relative and can look better even when dollar errors are worse.
  - In your current script, the printed r2 is especially less important because it
    is an in-sample statistic from the fitted regime models, while RMSE is on
    held-out validation predictions.

  So for this repo:

  - Use RMSE to decide what to keep.
  - Treat r2 as a rough diagnostic only.

  Best next ideas from the literature/docs:

  - Spline terms for the biggest continuous drivers.
    Patsy supports B-spline bases via bs(...), which lets you stay in linear
    regression while fitting smooth nonlinear effects for variables like living
    area, basement area, and lot size. This is probably the highest-upside next
    step because it keeps OLS but relaxes the rigid shape assumption.
    Sources:
    https://patsy.readthedocs.io/en/v0.5.1/spline-regression.html
    https://patsy.readthedocs.io/en/latest/API-reference.html
  - Formula-based statsmodels/patsy design matrices.
    Statsmodels documents using Patsy formulas for richer design matrices,
    including interactions and spline bases, without hand-building dummy matrices.
    That would make it much easier to test compact nonlinear specifications
    cleanly.
    Source:
    https://www.statsmodels.org/stable/example_formulas.html
  - Box-Cox style functional-form search.
    Hedonic pricing literature explicitly treats functional form as a core
    problem, and Box-Cox or semilog variants are standard approaches. In our
    setting, that suggests trying power transforms of key predictors, not just the
    target.
    Sources:
    https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/0094119085900129
    https://www.researchgate.net/publication/283842709_Hedonic_Regression_Models
  - Better use of the Ames variable structure.
    De Cock’s Ames paper emphasizes the mix of nominal, ordinal, discrete, and
    continuous predictors. That supports trying a more principled encoding split:
    ordered quality variables as structured numeric terms, continuous size
    variables with splines, and nominal geography still as factors.
    Source:
    https://jse.amstat.org/v19n3/decock.pdf
    Dataset reference:
    https://modeldata.tidymodels.org/reference/ames.html

• Outliers are still one of the few high-upside levers left, but the earlier tests
  were too generic. The next ideas should be more surgical and model-aware.

  Best outlier ideas to try:

  - Segment-specific outlier trimming.
    Don’t trim globally. Compute influence separately in the low- and high-quality
    regimes and remove only the top 1-3 points inside each regime.
    Why: the high-end segment likely has legitimate expensive homes that look like
    “outliers” globally but are normal within that market.
  - Trim on log-space residuals, not raw-price influence.
    Since the model is trained on log1p(SalePrice), define outliers using absolute
    residuals in log space, or Cook’s distance from the log-target fits.
    Why: this matches the actual optimization target better than raw-dollar
    extremes.
  - One-sided trimming only for underfit extremes.
    Check whether the worst points are mostly massive underpredictions or
    overpredictions.
    If the model is systematically missing a small set of luxury homes, remove
    only the most extreme positive residuals in the high-end regime.
    Why: symmetric trimming may throw away useful structure.
  - Trim only when influence and rarity agree.
    Example rule:
    remove a point only if it has top-k Cook’s distance and belongs to a rare
    neighborhood/feature combination.
    Why: this avoids deleting influential but representative homes.
  - Winsorize predictors instead of dropping rows.
    For variables like LotArea, GrLivArea, TotalBsmtSF, clip to train-only upper
    quantiles separately by regime.
    Why: you keep data volume while reducing leverage from extreme predictor
    values.
  - Add explicit “extreme property” flags instead of deleting.
    Example:
    HugeLot, HugeLivingArea, LuxuryNeighborhood, ManyFireplaces
    Why: sometimes the outlier isn’t noise; it just needs a separate slope/
    intercept cue.
  - Hybrid approach: clip predictors, then trim top 1-2 influence points.
    That’s often better than pure deletion because most leverage is removed by
    clipping, and only the truly destabilizing rows are dropped.
  - Neighborhood-aware trimming.
    Within each neighborhood, standardize SalePrice or residuals and only flag
    points far from their local neighborhood distribution.
    Why: a 500k house is not equally anomalous in every neighborhood.

  Most promising concrete experiments:

  1. Regime-specific top-2 Cook’s distance trimming on the current model.
  2. Regime-specific top-2 absolute log-residual trimming.
  3. Regime-specific winsorization of LogLotArea, LogGrLivArea, and LogTotalBsmtSF
     at the 99th percentile.
  4. High-end-only trimming of the top 2 positive residuals.
  5. Add HugeLot and HugeLivingArea flags instead of trimming.

  My guess: the best chance is not aggressive deletion, but regime-specific
  winsorization + tiny high-end trim. That is more likely to improve RMSE without
  wrecking the validation distribution.
